Getting Started

Your digital profile isn’t just your search history. It includes your passwords, intimate emails, contacts, location history, the metadata of your daily life (dates, times, durations) and a whole lot more. Your data can be sold to brokers and collected by governments, used to surveil you, exposed in breaches and used to sell to you.

You should control what personal data you share and who you share it with.

By reducing the amount of data you leak unknowingly or give away freely without thinking, you can limit the effects of data collection.

If this is new to you, start with Understanding Your Digital Profile then go straight to 10 Quick Digital Privacy Wins and begin reclaiming your digital privacy.

Below that are more detailed steps to greater privacy.

What is currently happening?

You open an account to order some ink cartridges, book a train ticket, or reserve a table at a restaurant. You use a Gmail account to receive the confirmation and pop a reminder in your calendar app.

In a matter of minutes you’ve just handed over your name, home address, email, phone number, location and card details to the retailer, the email provider, and data brokers.

Within a year, this data could easily be on 50+ databases, where they can start to compile a Digital Profile that can then be sold on to advertisers, insurers, and governments. And if any single company or organisation in this chain suffers a breach, your personal details are exposed. Now think of how many times you’ve done that — hundreds. That’s a lot of data and personal information.

What can your Digital Profile include?

  • Personal details (Name, DOB, Nationality, Address)
  • Location history (Favourite routes, current location)
  • Behavioral data (Search keywords, medical queries, hobbies)
  • Communication logs (Emails, chat logs, contact lists)
  • Device fingerprints (Screen size, browser type, active times)
  • Photos (Face, eyes, fingerprints, mood, location metadata)

Why does this matter?

You might argue you have nothing to hide or you’re not important. Well, hackers or scammers don’t care for starters. But what if a political party gets in power in the future that isn’t keen on your comments, posts or activities? Or even worse, you already live in or visit a country that punishes or harms you for your online activity?

Yes, it’s your data. You’re very welcome to hand it out as you feel, but would you broadcast your search history and browsing, medical conditions, intimate messages or fringe views to strangers in the street? Would you let people see what you watch on telly or see what you’re up to behind closed doors? Would you tell them where you go every day, at what time and for how long?

It’s your data, your privacy and your right.

Core Privacy

A deeper dive into the tools and habits that give you real control. Take it at your own pace.

What is it? DNS translates human-readable web addresses (e.g. www.google.com) into machine-readable IP addresses (e.g. 142.250.190.4). By default, your Internet Service Provider (ISP) handles this, allowing them to see every website you visit.

What about privacy? Without a privacy-focused DNS, your ISP and default DNS providers build a complete log of your browsing history. This data can be sold to advertisers, used to throttle your connection speeds, or handed over to authorities without a warrant in some jurisdictions. The default DNS providers are usually Google’s 8.8.8.8 or Cloudflare’s 1.1.1.1.

We recommend NextDNS — a privacy-focused, configurable DNS provider that blocks ads, trackers, and malware at the network level.

  • Visit NextDNS.io and create a free account.
  • Set up a profile for each specific device (Mobile, Laptop, Router).
  • On your mobile and laptop download the NextDNS app (Android/iOS/Windows/Mac) and log in.
  • Follow the simple Setup Guide for the specific device and operating system.
  • Set it up on your router for whole-home coverage — log in to your router and enter your NextDNS ID into the DNS settings (usually under WAN/Internet settings).
  • On your preferred browser go to Settings > Network (or similar), enable “Use Secure DNS”, select “Custom” and enter your NextDNS URL.
  • Tip: Start with conservative blocklists. If a website breaks, add its domain to the Allowlist in your NextDNS dashboard (e.g. example.com, not the full URL).
  • IP Leak — after setting up your private DNS, test to see if it’s leaking.

Recommended providers

  • NextDNS
  • Quad9 DNS
  • Cloudflare DNS

Benefits

  • Significantly faster browsing as ad-tracking requests are blocked before they load.
  • Your ISP can no longer see the specific websites you visit.
  • Bypasses ISP-level censorship.

What is it? A VPN creates an encrypted tunnel between your device and a VPN server. All your internet traffic passes through this server, masking your real IP address and location from the websites you visit.

What about privacy? Without a VPN, your ISP can see every unencrypted site you visit and can throttle your speed for specific activities like streaming. On public Wi-Fi, anyone on the same network can intercept your data — passwords, emails — if the site doesn’t use HTTPS.

We recommend Proton VPN — open-source, based in Switzerland with strong privacy laws, and a strict no-logs policy.

  • Download the app from Proton.me/vpn.
  • Create a free account — no credit card required.
  • Install the app on your phone and computer.
  • Connect to the “Fastest” server or a specific country.
  • Tip: Add the VPN to your easy access menu so you can switch it on and off quickly — some sites (e.g. banking apps) can block VPN connections.
  • IP Leak — after setting up your VPN, test to see if it’s leaking your location.

Recommended providers

  • Proton VPN
  • Brave VPN
  • Mullvad

Benefits

  • Secure browsing on public Wi-Fi (cafes, airports).
  • Your ISP and local network can no longer see what you are doing, only that you are connected to a VPN.
  • Access to geo-blocked content and prevents ISP throttling based on your activity.

What is it? Passwords are the first line of defence. Weak or reused passwords are the cause of 80% of data breaches. A good password manager and two-factor authentication (2FA) app makes online life easier and more secure.

What about privacy? If you use an easy to guess password (e.g. Password123) or reuse a password, a single breach on one website can lead to attackers trying that same password on your bank, email, and social media. SMS-based 2FA (those 6 digit confirmation codes) is also insecure and vulnerable to SIM-swapping attacks.

We recommend Proton Pass — end-to-end encrypted, easy to use and synced between phones and laptops.

  • Download Proton Pass.
  • Add login details and new passwords as you go — don’t try to change all your passwords in one go.
  • Add the browser extension to your desktop and auto-fill passwords.
  • For greater security download Proton Authenticator and set up 2FA for critical accounts (Email, Bank, Social). You’ll need to go into Settings for each app to find 2FA.
  • Tip: Password managers often copy passwords to your clipboard. Enable “Clear clipboard after X seconds” to prevent other apps from reading it.

Recommended providers

  • Proton Pass
  • Bitwarden

Benefits

  • Eliminates the need to remember passwords — saves time and is super convenient.
  • Every account uses a unique randomly generated password. If one is stolen, the others remain secure.

Big Tech

Google, Microsoft and Meta have multiple products and data streams. These sections are about limiting their reach across all of them.

Google is the largest online data collector. They track your location, search history, YouTube views, Android phone usage — they read your emails, text messages, private documents, calendar, photos, what apps you install and a whole lot more. All of it used to build a comprehensive digital profile to target you with ads.

How to limit Google’s reach

  • Check Your Data: Visit Google Takeout to download and review what they know about you.
  • Remove Content: Use Google’s Removal Tool to remove personal info from search results.
  • Check out the alternative apps at privacypack.org.
  • Give this comprehensive article a read — How to De-Google List.

Windows 10/11, Edge, Hotmail/Outlook and OneDrive all collect extensive data — including typing patterns, conversations, document and photo contents, location, file metadata and browsing habits. Microsoft also holds your BitLocker encryption keys on their servers by default, giving them (and potentially law enforcement) access without your say.

How to limit Microsoft’s reach

  • Limit data collection: Go to Settings > Privacy & Security. Turn off “Activity History”, “Diagnostic data” and “Tailored Experiences”.
  • Follow this tutorial to access BitLocker and remove Microsoft’s access to your encryption keys.
  • Reduce bloat and excess data telemetry on your Windows laptop by downloading and running privacy.sexy.
  • Move sensitive files to a local drive, disable OneDrive or use an encrypted cloud service.
  • Consider using Linux — an entirely different operating system (requires some technical know-how).

Meta can track you across the web and collect a tonne of metadata — who you talk to, when, where and for how long — from WhatsApp, Facebook and Messenger, even if the content is encrypted. This metadata is used for advertising and profiling. They build “shadow profiles” of everyone you interact with, even if they don’t have an account, and use this data to micro-target ads and influence your online behaviour.

How to limit Meta’s reach

  • Limit data collection on your phone by revoking permissions for Contacts, Location and Microphone in app settings.
  • In Settings on Facebook and WhatsApp, check for privacy and security settings that are switched on by default and turn them off.
  • Move to a privacy respecting messaging app like Signal — it doesn’t collect metadata or have access to your chats.
  • Keep an eye out for Meta AI, especially default enabled smart features in settings.

Staying Private

Privacy is not a one-time setup. These habits keep it working.

Privacy is not a one-time setup. It requires regular checks to ensure settings haven’t reverted and new threats are addressed. Don’t be overwhelmed — build habits that stick and take your time to implement changes.

Monthly Checklist

  • Review Permissions: Check phone and desktop app permissions (Location, Camera, Mic). Revoke unused ones.
  • Check for Breaches: Run your emails through Have I Been Pwned. Change passwords immediately if breached.
  • Update Settings: After OS updates, re-check privacy toggles (AI, Diagnostics, Smart Features).
  • Clean Up: Delete unused apps — they collect data even when not in use.
  • Clipboard: Clear your phone and desktop clipboard regularly.
  • Recent Files: Search your phone for and delete old screenshots, downloads and edits. These often contain personal data you forgot about.
  • Add a recurring reminder to your calendar every 3 months to: delete unused apps, review app permissions and settings, check Have I Been Pwned, unsubscribe from unwanted emails and change any compromised passwords.
